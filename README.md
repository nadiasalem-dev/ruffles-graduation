# Ruffles Graduation Ceremony (p5.js)

This project is a small animated graduation ceremony built with p5.js.

The goal of the project is to explore animation, storytelling, and accessible design in a creative coding environment. The scene features a graduation stage, audience, announcer captions, and a determined graduate named Ruffles.

Accessibility considerations include:

- color-blind friendly color choices  
- captioned announcements  
- simple readable visuals  

Along the way, observant viewers may notice a few familiar characters in the audience.

## Why I Created This Project

After graduating from **California State University, Bakersfield** with my **Computer Science degree**, I spent about a year trying to find work in the field. During that time I decided to pursue a master’s degree in **Assistive Technology** at **California State University, Northridge**.

At the time I was somewhat burned out from coding and wasn’t sure whether I would be accepted into either **Assistive Technology Engineering** or **Assistive Technology Studies and Human Services**. I applied to the Studies and Human Services program and was accepted.

I graduated in **December 2025**, and somewhere along the way I rediscovered my enjoyment of coding.

This project became a way to celebrate that. I decided to create my own graduation ceremony through code. It may not be my real ceremony, and the graduates are represented by cats inspired by my own, but in its own way it still feels like **my ceremony**.

## Graduates

The ceremony features **18 different cat graduates**, each created from a sprite sheet and designed to look slightly different from the others.

Each cat represents a member of the **real-life cohort I am graduating with**, turning the animation into a small tribute to the group.

The final graduate, **Ruffles**, represents me.

---

## Ceremony Flow

Each graduate walks across the stage toward the podium.

When a cat reaches the podium, a banner appears displaying the graduate’s **name** and whether they received the **Distinction award**. Currently this distinction status is randomly assigned, though this may later be updated if the real-life cohort provides the actual results.

The graduate pauses briefly at the podium before continuing off stage.

The final graduate, **Ruffles**, behaves slightly differently. She approaches the podium at a faster pace, receives the special award **“Distinction (close enough)”**, pauses, and then slowly walks off the stage.

On narrower screens Ruffles may bump into the previous graduate while approaching the podium. While this originally happened due to screen-width timing differences, it will likely become an intentional feature in a future version where Ruffles bumps the previous graduate on **all screen sizes**.

---

## Sprite Animation

All graduates are drawn from a **single sprite sheet** containing the cat images.

The sprite sheet is organized into **3 rows and 6 columns**, giving a total of **18 different cats**, one for each member of the real-life cohort.

Each cat’s image is extracted using calculated coordinates that select the correct portion of the sprite sheet. The values are carefully chosen so the program captures **only the intended cat** and avoids including parts of the surrounding images above, below, or beside it.

---

## Audience and Hidden Character

The ceremony includes an audience section generated by a function that draws **cat silhouettes** arranged in **lecture-style seating** behind the stage.

Each silhouette represents a simple audience member and helps create the feeling of a full ceremony without adding unnecessary visual complexity.

One seat in the audience is **randomly selected** and replaced with an image of a **red-striped zebra named Zoey**.

Zoey was originally intended to appear larger and more noticeable, but when she was changed from a silhouette to a full image, the idea evolved into a small hidden detail. Viewers who look closely may be able to spot her in the audience, turning the scene into a subtle **“Where’s Zoey?”** moment.

This concept may later be expanded into a **future interactive design idea**, where viewers actively search for Zoey in the crowd.

## Accessibility Considerations

Color choices for the **stage, banner, and audience area** were selected with **color-blind viewers in mind**, aiming for combinations that remain distinguishable without relying solely on color differences.

The audience includes a hidden character, **Zoey**, who appears as a small red-striped zebra among the cat silhouettes. Because Zoey is intentionally small, she may be difficult for low-vision viewers to spot. At the moment she functions mainly as an **easter egg**, and the scene can be **zoomed in** if someone wants to search for her.

All graduate information is currently presented in **written form on the banner**, ensuring that names and awards are visible without requiring audio.

Future versions of the project are planned to include:

- an **announcer voice** that introduces each graduate  
- spoken details including the **graduate’s name, major, and award status**  
- a special spoken moment for **Ruffles’ “Distinction (close enough)”** award  
- a unique ending reflecting **Ruffles’ stubborn personality**

The announcer will be paired with **prewritten closed captions** so that all spoken information remains accessible to viewers who rely on text.

## Technical Highlights

### State-Based Animation

Each graduate is controlled using a **state system** that determines what the cat is currently doing during the ceremony.

Examples of states include:

- **walkingToPodium** – the graduate moves toward the stage podium  
- **pausing** – the graduate waits while their name and award are displayed  
- **leaving** – the graduate exits the stage after their announcement  
- **refuseToLeave** – a special case used when a graduate stubbornly remains at the podium

Using explicit states keeps the animation logic organized and makes it easier to add new behaviors without rewriting the movement system.

---

### Sequential Graduate Timing

Graduates move across the stage from **left → podium → right**.

When one cat reaches the podium and enters the **pause** state, the next cat begins walking toward the stage. This creates a continuous ceremony where graduates appear one after another rather than all moving at once.

Most graduates move at the same walking speed. **Ruffles**, however, moves faster toward the podium before pausing and leaving more slowly, giving the final graduate a slightly different stage presence.

A future version will introduce a **security cat** who approaches from the opposite direction (**right → podium → right**) to escort Ruffles away from the stage.

---

### Sprite Sheet Extraction and Debugging

All graduates are drawn from a single **sprite sheet** arranged in rows and columns. In theory, the image could be divided evenly using the row and column layout.

In practice, this approach did not work perfectly because the sprites were not aligned cleanly enough for exact mathematical division. Instead, the sprite width (`sw`) and sprite height (`sh`) values had to be **manually approximated and adjusted** to correctly isolate each cat.

This required several rounds of debugging and visual testing. Small errors in the cropping values would cause artifacts from nearby sprites to appear. For example, what initially looked like a shadow under a cat turned out to be **the graduation cap of the cat in the row below**.

Through repeated adjustment and testing, the cropping values were refined until each sprite displayed the correct graduate without including unwanted parts of neighboring images.

---

### Responsive Layout and Screen Scaling

The animation was originally built using **hard-coded values** for screen size and element placement. Positions for the stage, podium, banner, and other elements were manually chosen based on how the scene looked on a desktop screen.

This approach worked initially but caused problems when the project was viewed on different devices. When the animation was opened on a **phone screen**, the layout no longer scaled correctly.

To fix this, the layout was refactored to use **relative positioning based on the canvas `width` and `height`**. Instead of fixed numbers, many values are now calculated using **multiplicative proportions of the screen size**.

This change allows the ceremony scene to adapt more naturally across different screen sizes while keeping the stage layout consistent.

## Future Plans

Several additional features are planned to expand the animation and add more personality to the ceremony:

- **Cap Toss Finale**  
  Zoey will toss the first graduation cap, followed by other caps appearing in the air as the celebration spreads across the scene.

- **Ruffles’ Dramatic Exit**  
  After her moment at the podium, Ruffles will be **dragged off stage by a tuxedo cat wearing a security outfit**, emphasizing her stubborn tendency to stay at the podium too long.

- **Coffee Mug Details**  
  The podium currently includes a **coffee mug** belonging to the computer presenting the diplomas. A future update will add **animated steam** rising from the mug.

- **Announcer Voice**  
  A narrator will introduce each graduate using a **computer-style voice performed by the developer**. The announcements will include the graduate’s name, major, and award status, and will be paired with **closed captions**.

- **Ruffles Bumping the Previous Graduate**  
  Ruffles currently bumps the previous cat on narrow screens due to timing differences. A future update will make this **intentional behavior on all screen sizes**, with Ruffles arriving before the previous graduate has fully left the podium.

- **Graduate Shadows**  
  Each graduate will eventually have a **shadow beneath them** to give the characters more depth and visual grounding on the stage.
